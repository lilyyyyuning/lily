Impressionism

A reaction to Romantic grandeur, this style “painted” with sound.
Hazy textures, exotic scales, and fluid rhythms,
evoked atmosphere, light, and nature—mirroring Monet’s art.

Claude Debussy

(1862–1918)


A Parisian rebel who hated the term "Impressionism," Debussy drew inspiration from Javanese gamelan, nature, and symbolist poetry. His bohemian lifestyle sparked scandals.

Famous works: Clair de Lune, La Mer, Prelude to the Afternoon of a Faun.
Unique features: "Sonic watercolors"—fluid harmonies, whole-tone scales, and shimmering textures that evoke moods rather than narratives.

Maurice Ravel

(1875–1937)


A meticulous perfectionist inspired by dance and machinery, Ravel served in WWI and battled neurological illness. His Basque heritage and love of Spain infused works like Boléro.

Famous works: Boléro, Daphnis et Chloé, Pavane for a Dead Princess.
Unique features: Clockwork precision; jazz-inflected harmonies; orchestral brilliance. Boléro’s hypnotic repetition builds to a crushing climax.

Erik Satie

(1866–1925)


An eccentric Parisian iconoclast, Satie lived in poverty, collecting umbrellas and writing absurdist score instructions ("like a nightingale with a toothache"). Café pianist by day, mystic by night.

Famous works: Gymnopédies, Gnossiennes, Parade.
Unique features: Minimalist "furniture music" (background sound); haunting modal melodies; stripped of Romantic excess. Precursor to ambient music.
