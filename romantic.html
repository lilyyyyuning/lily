Romantic

Emotion reigned supreme.
Composers poured personal passion, nationalism, and nature-inspired imagery,
into expansive symphonies, virtuosic piano works, and operas.
Orchestras swelled; harmonies grew lush and chromatic.

Frédéric Chopin

(1810–1849)


The "Poet of the Piano" left Poland for Paris, where his delicate health and doomed romance with writer George Sand fueled his melancholy. His funeral featured Mozart’s Requiem.

Famous works: Nocturnes, Ballades, Revolutionary Étude.
Unique features: Exquisite rubato (flexible tempo); singing melodies; innovative harmonies. Elevated the piano to an orchestra of intimate colors.

Franz Liszt

(1811-1886)


The quintessential Romantic virtuoso, Liszt’s flamboyant life included celebrated romances and a shift from dazzling pianist to innovative composer. After retiring from concertizing, he took minor holy orders, becoming the "Abbé Liszt."

Famous works: Hungarian Rhapsodies, Transcendental Études, Sonata in B Minor.
Unique features: Revolutionary piano technique; thematic transformation; invented the symphonic poem. Created orchestral power and texture at the keyboard.

Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky

(1840–1893)


Tormented by his sexuality in conservative Russia, Tchaikovsky channeled raw emotion into music. Patroness Nadezhda von Meck’s financial support saved him—though they never met.

Famous works: Swan Lake, 1812 Overture, Symphony No. 6 "Pathétique".
Unique features: Sweeping, heart-wrenching melodies; lush orchestration; ballet scores that defined the genre.
