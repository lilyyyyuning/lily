Baroque

Dramatic, ornate, and emotionally intense, Baroque music prized contrast and virtuosity.
Think intricate counterpoint, sparkling harpsichords, and grand sacred works.
It birthed opera and orchestral foundations.

Johann Sebastian Bach

(1685–1750)


Born into a German musical dynasty, Bach was a devout Lutheran who worked as a church organist, court musician, and Leipzig’s Thomaskantor. He fathered 20 children (including C.P.E. and J.C. Bach) and weathered professional clashes. His music fused mathematical precision with profound spirituality.

Famous works: Brandenburg Concertos, Mass in B Minor, The Well-Tempered Clavier.
Unique features: Master of counterpoint (interwoven melodies), intricate fugues, and chromatic harmonies that pushed Baroque conventions. His works became the bedrock of Western music theory.

Antonio Vivaldi

(1678–1741)


The "Red Priest" of Venice (named for his hair color) taught violin at an orphanage for girls, composing most of his works for their acclaimed orchestra. Despite fame, he died in poverty. His music radiates Venetian sunshine and virtuosity.

Famous works: The Four Seasons, Gloria, L'estro armonico.
Unique features: Pioneered the three-movement concerto form; driving rhythms, vivid programmatic storytelling (e.g., bird calls in "Spring"), and exuberant violin techniques.

George Frideric Handel

(1685–1759)


A cosmopolitan German who conquered London with Italian opera and English oratorios. Blinded late in life, he remained unmarried and famously intense (he nearly dueled a tenor). Queen Caroline’s funeral featured his anthem Zadok the Priest.

Famous works: Messiah ("Hallelujah Chorus"), Water Music, Music for the Royal Fireworks.
Unique features: Grand choral writing, dramatic harmonic shifts, and melodies of monumental simplicity. Blended German rigor, Italian lyricism, and English grandeur.
