Classical

Clarity, balance, and elegance defined this era.
Structured forms like the sonata and symphony flourished,
emphasizing melody over Baroque complexity.
Orchestras grew, and pianos replaced harpsichords.

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart

(1756–1791)


A child prodigy touring Europe at age six, Mozart chafed under Salzburg’s archbishop before achieving freelance fame in Vienna. His life blended genius, financial chaos, and mysterious death at 35.

Famous works: Requiem, The Marriage of Figaro, Symphony No. 40.
Unique features: "Perfect" balance of melody and form; effortless shifts between comedy and tragedy; crystalline orchestration. His operas revolutionized character psychology.

Joseph Haydn

(1732–1809)


The genial "Father of the Symphony" spent 30 years serving Hungary’s Esterházy family.
Isolated at their palace, he innovated relentlessly.
His humor surfaces in surprises like the Surprise Symphony’s loud chord.

Famous works: The Creation, Emperor Quartet, Trumpet Concerto.
Unique features: Architect of sonata form and the string quartet; witty musical jokes; folk-inspired melodies. Mentored Mozart and taught Beethoven.

Ludwig van Beethoven

(1770–1827)


Defiant against growing deafness and personal turmoil, Beethoven transformed from a Classical virtuoso to Romantic revolutionary. His "Heiligenstadt Testament" reveals despair overcome by artistic resolve.

Famous works: Symphony No. 9 ("Ode to Joy"), Moonlight Sonata, Fidelio.
Unique features: Shattered formal limits with explosive dynamics; personal expression over elegance; motivic development (building whole movements from tiny themes).
